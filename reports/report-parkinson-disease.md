**Druggability:** LRRK2 is highly druggable, being a protein kinase with a wealth of existing 3D structures and 54 known bioactive compounds, some of which have reached Phase 4 clinical development.

**Literature:** Mutations in the Leucine-Rich Repeat Kinase 2 (LRRK2) gene are recognized as one of the most frequent genetic causes of Parkinson's disease (PD). LRRK2 contributes to PD pathogenesis through mechanisms such as increased kinase activity, imbalance in protein homeostasis, and involvement in alpha-synuclein aggregation. Furthermore, LRRK2 genotype can influence the immune and transcriptomic responses in PD patients, particularly in the context of viral infections like human pegivirus.

| Protein Class | Belongs to the synuclein family |
| 3D Structure | Yes |
| Known Compounds | 66 |
| Max Drug Phase | 4 |
| PubMed Papers | 242 |
| Evidence | supporting |

**Druggability:** Despite not being a classical enzyme or receptor, the extensive structural data and the existence of compounds, including one in Phase 4, suggest SNCA is a tractable target, likely through modulation of its aggregation propensity rather than a traditional active site.

**Literature:** SNCA is strongly implicated in Parkinson's disease (PD), with pathogenic mutations like p.A53V directly linked to human early-onset PD. The abnormal accumulation and impaired degradation of misfolded SNCA/α-synuclein, leading to toxic oligomer formation, are central to PD pathology. Consequently, therapeutic strategies for PD often focus on enhancing SNCA clearance or preventing its aggregation, highlighting SNCA as a critical target.

**Druggability:** ATP13A2 appears intrinsically druggable due to its identity as a membrane-bound ATPase with numerous available 3D structures, despite the current lack of known small molecule modulators, suggesting it's an underexplored but tractable target.

**Literature:** Mutations in ATP13A2 (PARK9) are a known genetic cause of early-onset parkinsonism, specifically Kufor-Rakeb Syndrome, and the gene is explicitly listed as a PD-related gene. ATP13A2 plays a critical role in lysosomal function and autophagy, with its dysfunction linked to impaired lysosomal acidification and degradation, mechanisms implicated in Parkinson disease pathogenesis. Decreased ATP13A2 levels have been observed in dopaminergic neurons of sporadic PD patients, suggesting its broader involvement and potential as a therapeutic target.

| 3D Structure | Yes |

| PubMed Papers | 96 |
| Evidence | supporting |

**Druggability:** Despite being an enzyme (E3 ubiquitin ligase) with multiple available 3D structures that enable structure-based design, the complete absence of known bioactive compounds or clinical candidates suggests this target currently poses significant challenges for conventional small molecule drug discovery.

**Literature:** Mutations in the PRKN (Parkin) gene are a well-established cause of early-onset Parkinson disease (EOPD). PRKN, in conjunction with PINK1, plays a critical role in mitophagy, a process essential for clearing damaged mitochondria, with dysfunction in this pathway contributing to neuronal degeneration. Genetic studies have identified specific pathogenic PRKN variants as frequent contributors to EOPD across diverse populations.

**Druggability:** PINK1, as a serine/threonine protein kinase with existing 3D structures and known small molecule modulators, including a Phase 4 compound (Niclosamide), appears highly druggable. Its mitochondrial and cytosolic localization also makes it accessible for targeting.

**Literature:** Mutations in the PINK1 gene are a known cause of early-onset Parkinson disease, highlighting its direct involvement in the disease's etiology. PINK1 is crucial for the PINK1-PRKN mediated mitophagy pathway, which is essential for clearing damaged mitochondria and preventing neuronal degeneration. Given that mitochondrial dysfunction and mitophagy deficits are hallmarks of Parkinson disease, PINK1 is considered a significant contributor to its pathogenesis and a potential therapeutic target, with PINK1 activators currently in clinical trials.

## Recommendation

LRRK2 and PINK1 are the most promising targets, demonstrating strong genetic association with Parkinson's disease, high intrinsic druggability as kinases, and existing compounds in Phase 4 clinical development. SNCA is also a high-priority target, central to PD pathology through alpha-synuclein aggregation, with modulators already in Phase 4 despite its non-classical nature. While ATP13A2 and PRKN are biologically relevant, the complete absence of known small molecule modulators presents a significant drug discovery risk. Therefore, we recommend immediate progression for LRRK2, PINK1, and SNCA, while initiating early lead identification campaigns for ATP13A2 and PRKN to explore their untapped therapeutic potential.
