**Druggability:** SLC2A1 is a druggable target given its cell membrane localization, multiple available 3D structures suitable for SBDD, and the existence of numerous known bioactive compounds, including several approved drugs that have reached Phase 4 in clinical development. Targeting membrane transporters can be challenging, but the existing progress suggests tractability.

**Literature:** Mutations in the SLC2A1 gene, which encodes the glucose transporter protein type 1 (GLUT-1), are the direct cause of GLUT-1 deficiency syndrome (GLUT1-DS). This rare neurometabolic disorder is characterized by impaired glucose transport into the brain, leading to symptoms such as infantile seizures, developmental delay, acquired microcephaly, and distinctive movement and gait abnormalities. Diagnosis is confirmed by genetic testing for SLC2A1 mutations, and a ketogenic diet is the primary treatment strategy.

## Recommendation

**Recommendation:**

SLC2A1 is unequivocally the most promising drug target for GLUT-1 deficiency syndrome, given its direct causal role in the disease etiology and established druggability. Its high association score (0.852) and extensive literature confirm that mutations in SLC2A1 directly impair brain glucose transport. While targeting this membrane transporter carries risks of systemic off-target effects, SLC2A1's structural data, existing bioactive compounds, and approved drugs that have reached Phase 4 indicate therapeutic tractability. We recommend prioritizing the development of specific, brain-penetrant modulators for SLC2A1, followed by rigorous preclinical validation, as other identified targets lack direct disease association.
