**Druggability:** SCN1A is a highly druggable target, being a well-characterized cell membrane ion channel with existing 3D structural data. The presence of numerous known bioactive compounds, including approved drugs (Max Phase 4), confirms its established tractability and therapeutic relevance.

**Literature:** SCN1A mutations are a well-established genetic cause of hemiplegic migraine (HM), specifically familial hemiplegic migraine type 3 (FHM3), alongside CACNA1A and ATP1A2. These mutations are typically missense and lead to a gain-of-function of the NaV1.1 sodium channel, causing neuronal hyperexcitability and facilitating cortical spreading depolarization, which is the underlying mechanism of the migraine aura. Genetic testing for SCN1A is used to confirm the diagnosis of HM.

**Druggability:** ATP1A2 is a highly druggable membrane-bound enzyme, evidenced by the existence of Phase 4 clinically approved compounds like digoxin and digitoxin targeting the Na+/K+-ATPase family. Although direct 3D structures for ATP1A2 are currently unavailable, its functional importance and known modulation by existing drugs confirm its therapeutic tractability.

**Literature:** Mutations in the ATP1A2 gene are a well-established cause of Familial Hemiplegic Migraine type II (FHM2), a severe subtype of migraine with aura characterized by transient motor weakness. These mutations affect the Na+/K+-ATPase, leading to ion transport dysfunction and facilitating cortical spreading depolarization, which underlies the migraine aura. While primarily linked to FHM2, some ATP1A2 mutations can also be associated with more profound neurological phenotypes, including developmental and epileptic encephalopathies and polymicrogyria.

**Druggability:** CACNA1A appears highly druggable as a membrane-bound calcium channel with existing 3D structures and a known ligand, apinocaltamide, having reached Phase 2 clinical trials.

**Literature:** Pathogenic variants in the CACNA1A gene are a well-established genetic cause of hemiplegic migraine (HM), a severe subtype of migraine characterized by motor weakness and aura. Mutations in CACNA1A, which encodes a Ca2+ channel, contribute to altered ion transport and cortical spreading depolarization, underlying the pathophysiology of HM. Hemiplegic migraines are a common and significant phenotype observed in individuals with CACNA1A-related channelopathies.

**Druggability:** As a secreted peptide hormone, CALCA acts as a ligand for its receptor (CALCR), making CALCR the more conventional druggable target rather than CALCA itself. The absence of known compounds or clinical progress further suggests limited direct druggability for CALCA.

**Literature:** CALCA is strongly linked to migraine, identified as a genetic risk locus in large genome-wide association studies. It plays a crucial role in migraine pathophysiology by encoding calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), a key neurovascular mediator and target for migraine-specific drugs. Additionally, CALCA's genetic variations and epigenetic patterns are associated with migraine symptoms, age of onset, and individual responses to treatment.

**Druggability:** CALCB, as a secreted peptide hormone with no known 3D structures and only early-stage compounds (max phase 0), presents significant challenges for direct small-molecule druggability. Its nature as a peptide makes it a poor target for conventional small molecules, though its receptor (CALCRL-RAMP1) is a more tractable GPCR target.

**Literature:** Genetic studies, including genome-wide association studies and Mendelian randomization analyses, consistently identify CALCB as a risk locus robustly associated with an increased risk of migraine. CALCB encodes the calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP), which is widely recognized as a proven and actively developed therapeutic target for migraine, underscoring its critical role in migraine pathophysiology.

## Recommendation

*SCN1A*, *ATP1A2*, and *CACNA1A* are the most promising targets due to their strong genetic association with severe familial hemiplegic migraine and established druggability, with existing compounds (Max Phase 2-4) validating their therapeutic potential by modulating ion channel/pump function. These targets directly address neuronal hyperexcitability and cortical spreading depolarization, central to migraine pathophysiology, though potential off-target effects and specificity for severe migraine subtypes represent key risks. Conversely, while *CALCA* and *CALCB* (CGRP-encoding genes) highlight a highly validated migraine pathway, their direct druggability as peptides is low, making their receptors a more conventional drug target for novel small molecules. Therefore, we recommend prioritizing further investigation into selective small-molecule modulators for *SCN1A*, *ATP1A2*, and *CACNA1A*, while continuing to leverage insights from the established CGRP pathway for broader migraine indications.
