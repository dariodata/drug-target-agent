**Druggability:** DRD2 is a highly druggable target, being a well-characterized GPCR with numerous existing 3D structures and a long history of successful drug development, including multiple compounds reaching Phase 4 clinical trials (e.g., Clozapine).

**Literature:** Multiple large-scale genetic studies, including GWAS and meta-analyses, consistently demonstrate that DRD2 and its specific polymorphisms (e.g., rs1076560, rs1801028, rs1799978) are significantly associated with an increased risk for schizophrenia and its intermediate phenotypes. DRD2 associations are also noted in treatment-resistant schizophrenia and are considered relevant to known pathophysiological hypotheses and potential therapeutic targets. While some DRD2 polymorphisms show no significant association, others strongly support its role in the disorder.

## 2. SHANK3 — SH3 and multiple ankyrin repeat domains 3

| Metric | Value |

| Association Score | 0.702 |
| UniProt Accession | Q9BYB0 |
| Protein Class | Unknown |
| 3D Structure | Yes |
| Known Compounds | 0 |
| Max Drug Phase | 0 |
| PubMed Papers | 63 |
| Evidence | supporting |

**Druggability:** SHANK3, an intracellular scaffold protein primarily involved in protein-protein interactions, represents a challenging drug target due to its protein class and subcellular location. The complete absence of known small molecule modulators further suggests low druggability, despite the availability of some 3D structural data.

**Literature:** SHANK3 is a well-established risk gene for schizophrenia, with mutations and dysregulated expression contributing to the disorder's pathology. In first-episode schizophrenia patients, SHANK3 promoter hypermethylation is observed in peripheral blood mononuclear cells, correlating with symptom severity and cortical changes, and suggesting its role in neuropathology. Mouse models carrying schizophrenia-linked SHANK3 mutations exhibit adolescent sleep defects, dopaminergic hyperactivity, and altered dendritic spine dynamics, which are implicated in the development of adult social deficits relevant to schizophrenia.

**PMIDs:** 37211699, 37187620, 37144901

| 3D Structure | Yes |

| PubMed Papers | 14 |
| Evidence | inconclusive |

**Druggability:** Despite being a membrane receptor with existing 3D structures, the complete lack of known ligands or compounds in development suggests low conventional druggability for RTN4R, indicating significant challenges in identifying pharmacological modulators.

**Literature:** Multiple large case-control studies in Chinese and Korean populations found no significant association between RTN4R genetic polymorphisms and schizophrenia. However, in individuals with 22q11.2 deletion syndrome, an RTN4R SNP was linked to specific brain volumetric changes that predicted ultra high risk symptoms of psychosis. This suggests RTN4R's involvement may be specific to certain genetic contexts rather than a general susceptibility factor for schizophrenia.

**PMIDs:** 30004142, 16897606, 21377214

| PubMed Papers | 188 |

**Druggability:** DRD3 is a highly druggable target due to its classification as a GPCR (a major drug target class) located in the cell membrane, supported by 6 available 3D structures. The presence of 99 known compounds, with several reaching Phase 2 clinical trials, further validates its tractability for drug discovery.

**Literature:** DRD3 polymorphisms, particularly Ser9Gly, show a significant association with schizophrenia susceptibility in Caucasian and African populations, although findings are inconsistent in other ethnic groups like Malays. Furthermore, DRD3 polymorphisms are linked to phenotypic aspects of schizophrenia, including poor treatment response to antipsychotics and clozapine resistance, especially in Caucasians. This suggests a role for DRD3 in influencing both disease risk and treatment outcomes in specific populations.

**PMIDs:** 36980961, 35835396, 9674978

**Druggability:** MTHFR is an enzyme with available 3D structures, indicating high potential for rational drug design. However, the complete absence of known bioactive compounds suggests a lack of validated chemical starting points or significant challenges in identifying modulators.

**Literature:** Multiple meta-analyses consistently report that polymorphisms in the MTHFR gene, particularly the C677T variant, are associated with an increased risk of schizophrenia. This association is linked to the MTHFR enzyme's role in folate metabolism, potentially leading to hyperhomocysteinemia. While some studies suggest ethnic variations in this association and one study did not find a direct susceptibility link, it did note an association with cognitive impairment in schizophrenia patients.

## Recommendation

**Recommendation Section**

DRD2 is the most promising drug target for schizophrenia, exhibiting a strong association score (0.734), consistent genetic evidence, and high druggability as a GPCR with multiple compounds already in Phase 4 clinical trials (e.g., Clozapine). DRD3 is also highly druggable, with compounds reaching Phase 2 for its role in risk and treatment response, though ethnic variations in association require careful consideration. Conversely, SHANK3, despite robust genetic evidence and high association (0.702), presents significant druggability challenges as an intracellular scaffold protein lacking known modulators. We recommend prioritizing DRD2/DRD3 for lead optimization and exploring differentiated therapeutic mechanisms, while simultaneously initiating structural biology and novel modality discovery efforts for SHANK3 to overcome its inherent druggability limitations.
